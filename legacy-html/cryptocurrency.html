Cryptocurrency & Wallets

If blockchain is the ledger, cryptocurrency is the money written into it. Here's how value moves — and how you prove it's yours without a bank.

Coins vs. Tokens

People use these interchangeably, but there's a real difference:

Coin — the native currency of its own blockchain. Examples: BTC on Bitcoin, ETH on Ethereum. Used to pay network fees.
Token — built on top of an existing blockchain using a smart contract. Examples: USDC, UNI live on Ethereum. They piggyback on ETH's security.

Public & Private Keys — the heart of ownership

A crypto wallet doesn't actually "store coins." The coins live on the blockchain. Your wallet stores the keys that prove you control them. There are two, and the relationship is everything:

📬

Public key / address

Like your email address or account number. Share it freely so people can send you funds.

🔐

Private key

Like the password and signature combined. It proves ownership and authorizes transactions. Never share it.

"Not your keys, not your coins." Whoever holds the private key controls the funds — period. Lose it and the coins are gone forever; leak it and they can be stolen. There is no "forgot password" button.

Seed phrase (recovery phrase)

Because raw private keys are unwieldy, wallets give you a 12–24 word seed phrase that can regenerate all your keys. Write it on paper, store it offline, and never type it into a website. Anyone with these words owns your wallet.

Types of wallets

🔥 Hot wallet

Connected to the internet (e.g. MetaMask browser extension, mobile apps). Convenient for daily use, more exposed to hacks.

❄️ Cold wallet

Offline hardware device (e.g. Ledger, Trezor) or paper. Much safer for large, long-term holdings.

Anatomy of a transaction

When you send crypto, here's what actually happens:

  • 1. Create — you specify recipient address + amount.
  • 2. Sign — your wallet signs it with your private key (proving it's really you) without ever revealing the key.
  • 3. Broadcast — the signed transaction is sent to the network.
  • 4. Validate & confirm — validators/miners verify it and include it in a block. Once it's deep enough in the chain, it's irreversible.

What is "gas"?

Gas is the fee you pay to have the network process your transaction. It compensates the validators for computation and storage. Fees rise when the network is busy (more demand for limited block space) — like surge pricing. On Ethereum, gas is paid in ETH (often measured in tiny units called gwei).

Beginner gotcha: You need a little of the chain's native coin (ETH, etc.) to pay gas — even just to move a token like USDC.
