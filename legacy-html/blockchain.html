Blockchain Basics

A blockchain is a shared, append-only record book that thousands of computers keep in sync — with no boss in charge. Let's see how it stays trustworthy without anyone to trust.

What is a blockchain?

Imagine a notebook that records transactions. Instead of one person owning it, everyone in the network has an identical copy. When a new page (a block) is added, every copy updates. Because the pages are mathematically chained together, you can't secretly rip one out or edit it — that's what makes a blockchain tamper-evident.

Block — a bundle of data (e.g. transactions) plus a timestamp and a reference to the previous block.
Hash — a fixed-length digital fingerprint of data. Change the data even slightly and the hash changes completely.
Chain — each block stores the hash of the one before it, linking them into an ordered, unbreakable sequence.
Decentralization — no single server or company holds the data; it's distributed across many independent nodes.
Consensus — the rules nodes use to agree on which blocks are valid (e.g. Proof of Work, Proof of Stake).
Key idea: Trust in Web3 doesn't come from a company — it comes from math (hashing) and economics (consensus incentives).

How does everyone agree? (Consensus)

If thousands of computers each hold a copy, how do they agree on the next block? Through a consensus mechanism:

  • Proof of Work (PoW) — miners race to solve a hard math puzzle. Winner adds the block and earns a reward. Secure but energy-hungry. (Bitcoin)
  • Proof of Stake (PoS) — validators lock up ("stake") their coins for the right to add blocks. Cheat and you lose your stake. Far more energy-efficient. (Ethereum, since 2022)
Trade-off to remember: Blockchains balance the "trilemma" of decentralization, security, and scalability — improving one often pressures the others.
